My first week at my first real job, I found the gap in my education fast. I had a degree and an Excel course on my transcript, but college never offered a class on Outlook. And Outlook, it turned out, was how the entire company talked.

That memory has become my best explanation for why AI adoption is stalling inside organizations. We keep treating AI as an individual skill that each employee picks up on their own, when adoption has never worked that way. AI adoption is an organization learning a new **company language**, and almost nobody is teaching it like one.

## The Google Trap

There's a framing I hear constantly: AI is a skill you pick up like Googling. Nobody took a search class; you tried it, it worked, you kept doing it, and eventually everyone searched. AI, the argument goes, will spread the same way.

I understand the appeal. I also think it collapses the moment you apply it to an organization. Companies are not paying you to Google. No investment to justify, no rollout plan, no committee deciding search was strategic. Whether you used Google, Bing, or DuckDuckGo mattered to exactly nobody (your secret is safe, DuckDuckGo people). Search is a private skill with private benefits.

AI inside a company is the opposite. Your organization is paying real money for it, and the value only shows up when a group uses it together. Here's the tell: I can sit right next to you and use AI brilliantly all day, and it changes NOTHING about how you do your job. There is no correlation between my AI workflow and yours unless the company builds one. A skill spreads one person at a time. A language only works when everybody speaks it.

## Nobody Taught Me Outlook

Back to that first job. I had taken an Excel course in college, and I still had to relearn Excel on the ground, because the company's data lived in Excel files and there was a way things were done with them. Outlook was the bigger surprise. How to organize messages. How to send an email properly. Who gets copied, and what a calendar invite actually commits you to. None of that was in a syllabus, because it was never a subject. It was the company language: Excel for the data, Outlook for the messaging. If that company had run on Google Workspace instead, I would have learned Gmail and Sheets. Similar tools, different dialect, same lesson: speak the language or you cannot do the work.

Power BI became a language too, once we invested in it. There was a person talking and a person listening. Someone built the model and the report, someone consumed it, and both sides had to learn their part: where the source of truth lived, what validated meant, how a question became an answer.

That is what adoption actually is. Not a tool people have access to; a language a group agrees to speak, backed by a common process. Right now, AI in most organizations is a pile of private dialects. Everyone is fluent in their own agents, and nobody can talk to each other.

## AI-Ready Me Is Not AI-Ready Us

I would call myself AI-ready as an individual. I can deploy, manage, and apply AI across my own workflow, and I can measure whether it's working for me. Plenty of practitioners can say the same. So here's the uncomfortable question: what does AI-ready mean for an organization? For a team of people who have to produce something together?

Nobody has defined that yet. I think it's the critical gap. AI adoption is a culture thing: a group of people coming together to use the same tooling and the same process. My individual skills do not transfer to the org one-to-one, and neither do my agents.

Money doesn't close the gap either. You can invest heavily in AI tools and see barely any adoption, the same way publishing 200 Power BI reports never guaranteed anyone opened them.

Investment is a purchase. Adoption is a behavior.

Which is why so much of what I'm seeing right now is **AI theater**, not AI strategy. An AI solution wandering the halls looking for a problem. A copilot spun up because of a headline. The platform is rarely the blocker; between Fabric and semantic models, the building blocks for agents are already sitting there. Real strategy starts where it always starts: data governance, data quality, and the pain points your teams already have. If you're going to make an agent work in your company, it's going to run on YOUR data. So where do report requests still pile up? Where does someone re-key the same information every single week? Those pain points bridge naturally to something agentic, because the need is real and the data path already exists.

## The Decree Moment

Here's what real adoption looked like the first time I lived it. When we rolled out Power BI, sales leadership told every rep, in plain language: from now on, if you want to know your quota, you go here. This is the report. Do not go digging through your old Excel files. Your targets, your goals, everything is in here. And if you have a problem with it, message Tommy.

That was the beginning of adoption. Not the licensing. Not the tooling. The **decree**.

Now, that decree did not fall out of the sky. By the time leadership said those words, someone had already decided Power BI was worth the investment, security had gotten comfortable with it, and a report existed that had been developed and validated. The decree was the last domino, not the first. Does AI have that moment in most companies today? It does not. Most organizations haven't committed to tooling, haven't cleared security, haven't built anything worth decreeing. Then they wonder why nothing gets adopted.

So who owns getting there? Put AI enablement inside data governance. You already have the right people in the room: IT, business intelligence, and leadership with the purse strings. It's the natural body to decide what your company's AI language will be, and to line up the dominoes until a decree is possible.

## Treat the Agent Like a Hire

Once a team starts speaking the language, here's the standard I hold: treat AI for a department as a **first-class citizen**, the same way you would treat a new hire. What is its role? Who does it work with every day? AI does not work unless it's integrated into a team's normal workflow. An optional chat window off to the side is not a job.

And be honest about which kind of user you're serving. Someone using an app that AI built is not using AI. They're using a user interface, and they already know how to do that. An **agent consumer** is different. That person needs actual skill: prompting, knowing which agent to reach for, and when. I've built a stack of custom agents into my own Notion workspace. One organizes files, notes, and status updates. Another carries a set of Fabric skills. Drop a new teammate into that setup tomorrow and they would have no idea that changing a status triggers an agent, or that mentioning the operations agent pulls everything together. Those are things I know because I created them. That knowledge does not spread by osmosis. It spreads by training and shared process, exactly like Outlook etiquette did.

You'll know the language has landed when the agent shows up in the team's lingo. Did you talk to the quota agent today? Yeah, and here's what it said. When a team talks about an agent like a coworker, the agent is part of the team. That's adoption.

## A 30-60-90 That Starts With Data

If you asked me tomorrow for a 30-60-90 day plan to get started with AI, here's mine.

- **First 30 days: data quality.** Verify your data, and I mean both kinds. The hard data in your tables, and the soft data around it: definitions, process documents, the context an agent would need to be useful. Deliver findings on what's consistent and what's a mess. Scattered personal OneDrives and semantic models nobody cleaned are not the right data, and without the right data you're not going to have a great agent.
- **Days 30 to 60: discovery.** Find the team with good data and a genuine process need. Maybe it's operations, staring down the same filled-out forms week after week, sitting on data quality that's already solid. You're identifying candidates here, not building. The team you pick needs a clear reason for an agent to exist.
- **Days 60 to 90: one agent, in real use, by a real team.** Not a demo. Not a slide that says pilot. An agent wired into that team's actual workflow, with the training to go with it.

Three months for one agent sounds slow until you compare it to the alternative: twelve months of theater and zero adoption.

And before leadership can deliver your decree moment, check the dominoes:

1. Has someone with real authority decided which AI tooling the company is committing to?
2. Has security reviewed it and gotten comfortable?
3. Does a validated, working thing exist that's worth pointing people to?
4. Is there a named owner, an actual person to message when something breaks?

If any answer is no, that's your work. The decree comes last.

## Where This Goes

My opinion, plainly: the organizations that win with AI will not be the ones with the most tools or the loudest announcements. They will be the ones where leadership had the nerve to issue a decree and the patience to do the data work first. And my prediction: within a few years, AI-ready will stop being a line on an individual resume and become something we say about organizations, the way we talk about data culture today. Agent fluency will sit in onboarding right next to getting your Outlook account, and companies will teach it themselves, the way they have always taught their own language.
